In this episode of Melody Mind, Daniel and Annabelle explore a wide range of musical styles.

This episode talks about how composers changed not just sound, but also the way we listen to it. It starts with the end of classical music and goes to the start of modernism. Then, it talks about how minimalism went from being very loud to being very quiet. Finally, it talks about how post-classical and neo-classical music is very personal.

This isn't a history lesson.
It is a conversation about attention.

We start with the late works of Ludwig van Beethoven and Franz Schubert, where repetition and unresolved sounds quietly challenge the idea that music must always arrive somewhere clear. With Claude Debussy, harmony becomes less important, and atmosphere replaces direction.

Then, the conversation shifts to the confrontational energy of Igor Stravinsky and Arnold Schoenberg. Rhythm becomes physical force. Dissonance refuses resolution. Music stops reassuring its audience.

Minimalism is not just for decoration; it's a practical choice.
La Monte Young uses long-lasting sounds to create a sense of time.
Terry Riley builds structures of repetition for the community.
Steve Reich reveals how we perceive sound through patterns of phasing.

As minimalism moves from galleries into everyday life, the body becomes the focus. Philip Glass creates music that makes you feel like you can endure anything. Meredith Monk is a musician who uses the human voice in a way that is like a living instrument. Pauline Oliveros changes the idea of listening. She makes it an inclusive and political act.

The pathway then opens toward intimacy after classical music.
Max Richter focuses on emotional continuity rather than formal development.
Nils Frahm makes music that feels like it's right there in the room with you, like it's something you can almost touch.
